# WMRN Peanut Push: A Marion Holiday Tradition

The WMRN Peanut Push is one of Marion, Ohio’s most enduring community fundraising traditions. Established in 1969 through the initiative of WMRN broadcaster Charlie Evers, the Peanut Push reflects the station’s long-standing commitment to local civic engagement and holiday service.

More than a novelty event, the Peanut Push became an annual expression of community identity, public participation, and local radio leadership.

---

## Establishment (1969)

The Peanut Push was introduced in 1969 as part of WMRN’s expanding community-centered programming under Charlie Evers. Designed as a public fundraiser supporting the Junior Service Guild’s Christmas Clearinghouse, the event combined humor, spectacle, and charitable purpose.

Participants raise pledges and then push a peanut along a designated course — traditionally using only their nose — in a timed competition. What began as a creative radio promotion quickly evolved into a recurring civic ritual.

The tradition illustrates a broader era in local broadcasting when radio stations did more than report community life — they actively helped shape it.

---

## Purpose and Beneficiary

Funds raised through the Peanut Push benefit the Junior Service Guild’s Christmas Clearinghouse, which provides holiday assistance to local families in need.

By tying the event to seasonal giving, WMRN embedded the fundraiser within Marion’s holiday calendar. The Peanut Push became associated not only with entertainment, but with tangible community support and generosity.

---

## Event Structure and Participation

Over the decades, the Peanut Push has developed into a multi-part public event featuring:

- Individual and team competitions  
- Youth participation heats  
- Community organizations and local businesses  
- Station personalities serving as hosts and emcees  
- Public spectators and family attendance  
- Presentation of fundraising totals and ceremonial recognition  

In more recent years, the event has taken place in downtown Marion, including Founders Park, reinforcing its visibility within the city’s civic center.

Participants have ranged from students and civic leaders to local business representatives and WMRN on-air staff. The event’s accessible, playful format has contributed to its longevity.

---

## Fundraising Impact

Throughout its history, the Peanut Push has generated significant support for the Christmas Clearinghouse. Annual totals have reflected broad community participation, with local organizations and sponsors contributing alongside individual fundraisers.

The public presentation of ceremonial checks and fundraising “royalty” titles — such as King, Queen, Prince, or Princess — has added a structured and celebratory dimension to the tradition.

The Peanut Push remains one of the most recognizable local fundraising events associated with WMRN’s broadcast identity.

---

## Charlie Evers and Institutional Context

Charlie Evers, who began his radio career in 1949 and became a central on-air personality at WMRN in 1968, played a pivotal role in establishing the Peanut Push in 1969.

In the same year, Evers was also instrumental in developing Buckeye Chuck, Ohio’s official weather prognosticating groundhog. Together, these initiatives demonstrate a defining period in WMRN’s history when locally driven programming fostered enduring civic traditions.

The Peanut Push stands as a parallel community tradition to the Buckeye Chuck Groundhog Day broadcast — one rooted in charitable service rather than seasonal forecasting.

---

## Broadcast Integration

Historically, the Peanut Push has been supported and promoted through WMRN’s live programming. On-air promotion, event coverage, interviews, and post-event fundraising announcements reinforced the station’s role as both media outlet and community organizer.

The event exemplifies a mid-20th-century local radio model in which broadcasters served as civic catalysts and public conveners.
